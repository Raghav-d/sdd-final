Executive Summary

The Case for a Defined DE Role in PeopleTech

PeopleTech (PT) is an IFX Line of Business that builds and operates internal platforms — including AI/ML capabilities, HR system integrations, and corporate technology infrastructure — consumed by teams across Capital One. The engineering complexity of this work is unambiguous: it spans Workday, Salesforce, IoT devices, acquired company integrations, payroll and rewards systems, and a growing GenAI platform footprint.

IFX has a well-established Distinguished Engineer (DE) framework, anchored in four pillars: Uncompromising Operator, Talent Magnet, Creative Technologist, and Change Agent. Every other IFX Line of Business has mapped this framework to its domain. PeopleTech has not. This paper does that work — defining what a Distinguished Engineer does in PT, what scope is required for the role to deliver impact, and what structural conditions must be in place for that impact to be sustained.

The goal is not to advocate for any individual. It is to give PT leadership the vocabulary and structure to build durable technical leadership in a LOB that is increasingly central to Capital One's enterprise operations and workforce technology strategy.

01

PeopleTech Engineering: Beyond Configuration

"HR technology" is often perceived as SaaS configuration and vendor management. In Capital One's PeopleTech, that perception does not match reality.

PT builds and maintains internal platforms that other Lines of Business across Capital One depend on. These include an AI/ML platform supporting HR and corporate use cases, deep integrations between enterprise systems (Workday, Salesforce, payroll, rewards, L&D tooling), CorpTech infrastructure covering IoT devices and acquired company onboarding, and the engineering backbone for performance management, hiring, and workforce analytics.

The consumers of PT's platforms are not limited to HR — they span engineering, operations, finance, and risk functions enterprise-wide. This gives PT's technical decisions an outsized blast radius: architectural choices made here affect how Capital One's workforce operates, how data flows across people systems, and how AI-driven HR capabilities are built and governed.

The key distinction: PT is not a SaaS administration function. It is an internal platform engineering organization that happens to operate in the HR and corporate technology domain. The engineering challenges — scale, compliance, integration complexity, AI/ML — are structurally identical to those faced by any other Capital One LOB operating at this level.

Three characteristics define PT's engineering environment and make it particularly well-suited to DE-level technical leadership:

Regulatory and compliance exposure. PT systems touch SOX-relevant payroll and rewards data, HR privacy requirements, and audit-facing processes. Engineering decisions here carry compliance weight that demands rigorous non-functional requirements thinking — security, auditability, resilience — as first-class design concerns.

Cross-domain integration complexity. PT sits at the intersection of more enterprise systems than almost any other LOB. Workday, Salesforce, identity platforms, data platforms, IoT infrastructure, and third-party tools all require integration patterns that are coherent, reusable, and maintainable over time. Without architectural leadership, this complexity compounds into arbitrary uniqueness and technical debt.

Emerging AI/ML platform responsibility. PT is developing AI/ML capabilities for HR and corporate use cases that will be consumed enterprise-wide. This is platform engineering at scale — requiring the same architectural discipline, pattern governance, and strategic thinking as any Capital One technology platform.

02

The IFX DE Framework Applied to PeopleTech

The IFX Distinguished Engineer benchmark framework defines four value pillars. Each pillar is described below in its generic form, followed by a specific translation to PeopleTech's domain — grounding the framework in the actual work of the organization.

Pillar 01

Uncompromising Operator

Builds with scalability, security, automation, maintainability, and resilience as first-class concerns. Champions non-functional software qualities across the engineering org.

In PeopleTech, this means:

  • Defining and enforcing NFRs for Workday, Salesforce, and AI/ML platform integrations, including auditability and SOX-compliance requirements
  • Driving consistency in how PT systems are tested, logged, monitored, and deployed across HRTech, CorpTech, and HRBP
  • Leading architecture reviews for high-risk changes to payroll, rewards, and identity-adjacent systems
  • Establishing resilience and cost-effectiveness patterns for the AI/ML platform consumed enterprise-wide
Pillar 02

Talent Magnet

Serves as a beacon for engineering culture. Force-multiplies IC skills and work. Attracts, mentors, and grows diverse, values-aligned technologists.

In PeopleTech, this means:

  • Active mentorship in Capital One's DE 100 and DE 200 IC development programs — enterprise-level, not team-level
  • Coaching Domain Architects within PT on system design, architectural thinking, and technical decision-making
  • Representing PT engineering in cross-LOB technical communities and IFX architecture forums
  • Elevating the perception of PT as a serious engineering organization, countering the "SaaS config" narrative through demonstrated technical output
Pillar 03

Creative Technologist

Champions technology strategy, influences business strategy, and removes arbitrary uniqueness. Tackles problems where goals are moderately defined but technology strategy is ambiguous.

In PeopleTech, this means:

  • Defining the destination architecture for PT's AI/ML platform — what it is, what it enables, and how it connects to Capital One's enterprise GenAI strategy
  • Identifying and eliminating integration redundancy across the Workday, Salesforce, and CorpTech landscape
  • Translating HR and corporate business strategy into actionable technology initiatives with clear engineering roadmaps
  • Partnering with the GenAI COE to ensure PT's AI/ML work aligns with and contributes to enterprise common capability patterns
Pillar 04

Change Agent

Constructively disrupts status quo practices. Drives adoption of enterprise standards. Relentlessly seeks to automate and standardize. Brings others along.

In PeopleTech, this means:

  • Standardizing integration patterns across acquired company onboarding, reducing one-off solutions that accumulate as technical debt
  • Driving adoption of Capital One's enterprise architecture standards within PT's historically fragmented tool landscape
  • Constructively challenging the assumption that HR system complexity justifies bespoke architectural decisions
  • Communicating technical direction clearly across PT's three domains (HRTech, CorpTech, HRBP) and to non-technical business stakeholders
03

Scope and Boundaries

A defining characteristic of the Distinguished Engineer role is influence without authority. The DE does not manage people, own delivery timelines, or control budgets. The DE shapes technical direction through credibility, expertise, and the quality of their thinking. This distinction is critical to understanding both what the role is accountable for and what it depends on others to execute.

The table below defines what the PT DE owns, influences, and explicitly does not own.

Domain DE Owns / Is Accountable For DE Influences Outside DE Scope
Architecture Strategy Domain Destination Architecture (DDA) direction; cross-domain coherence; technical standards for PT IFX-level architecture forums; enterprise standards evolution Business strategy; product roadmap prioritization
AI/ML Platform Platform architecture direction; NFR definitions; integration with GenAI COE capability patterns GenAI COE roadmap; enterprise ML platform choices GenAI COE delivery execution; data science model development
Integration Standards Defining reusable integration patterns across Workday, Salesforce, CorpTech, and third-party systems Adoption velocity across Domain Architects; vendor selection criteria Individual project delivery; vendor contract negotiation
Compliance & Risk Architectural exception governance; anti-pattern identification; NFR compliance posture Cyber, Legal, and Audit engagement on PT systems Risk acceptance decisions (owned by business/SLT)
Talent Development Technical mentorship of Domain Architects; IC development through DE 100/200 programs Hiring bar for PT engineering roles; technical interview standards People management; performance ratings; compensation decisions
Engineering Culture Setting the technical bar; championing reuse, open source, and well-managed principles Cross-LOB engineering communities; IFX CoP participation Org design; team structure; resource allocation

The scope table above is a summary lens; the RACI is the operational detail.

The Application Architecture / Data Architecture Boundary

As PT's technical leadership model matures, one boundary requires explicit definition: the line between application and platform architecture (DE scope) and data architecture and governance (Data function scope). Ambiguity here creates overlap, friction, and contested ownership — particularly as AI/ML and data-intensive systems become more central to PT's work.

The principle is straightforward: the PT DE owns the architecture of systems that produce, consume, and move data — how those systems are structured, how they integrate, what non-functional requirements govern them, and how AI/ML capabilities are built on top of them. The Data function owns the data itself — its models, quality, lineage, governance standards, and the data platform layer that serves all of PT.

These are complementary scopes, not competing ones. In practice, they meet at specific interfaces:

Interface PT DE Owns Data Sr. Director Owns Engagement Model
AI/ML Platform Platform architecture, NFRs, system integration patterns Data models, feature stores, data quality standards feeding ML DE and Data Sr. Director co-design at the platform boundary; DE is Responsible, Data is Consulted on architectural decisions
Workday / HR System Integrations Integration architecture, API patterns, event-driven design HR data schema standards, data lineage, PII governance DE defines the integration pattern; Data defines what flows through it and how it is governed
Analytics & Reporting Platforms System architecture of the analytics platform itself Data models, metrics definitions, reporting governance DE is Consulted on analytics platform architectural choices; Data is Accountable for what the platform delivers
Data Platform Infrastructure Not in DE scope — consumption patterns only Accountable for data platform design and operation DE is Informed; engages as a consumer stakeholder, not an architect of the platform

On future DE roles in data: As PT's data engineering and AI/ML footprint grows, a dedicated DE embedded in the Data function is a natural evolution — focused on data platform architecture, ML infrastructure, and data engineering standards. That role would operate in the same IFX DE framework but with a data-domain mandate. The PT DE (application and platform scope) and a future Data DE would be peers — each owning their domain, collaborating at the interfaces defined above. This paper does not presuppose that structure; it defines the current boundary clearly so that future additions build on it rather than collide with it.

04

Structural Conditions for DE Effectiveness

The IFX DE framework is well-designed. But a framework applied to the wrong structure produces friction, not impact. Across Capital One LOBs where the DE role functions effectively, a consistent set of structural conditions is present. PT should be intentional about establishing these conditions.

↑
Reporting Altitude

The DE role operates most effectively with reporting visibility at Senior Director level or above. This is not a title requirement — it is a scope requirement. A DE scoped to a sub-domain reports a mismatch between title and mandate.

⊕
Cross-Pillar Access

PT's DE must have defined engagement with HRTech, CorpTech, HRBP, and the GenAI COE. Without cross-pillar access, the DE becomes an application architect for one team — a role mismatch that limits both the individual and the org.

◈
IFX Forum Participation

The DE should have a defined seat at IFX-level architecture and technology forums. PT's technical voice needs to be present where enterprise standards are shaped, not only where they are consumed.

≠
IC vs. People Leader Separation

The DE is an Individual Contributor role. Conflating it with people manager expectations — in scope definition, performance frameworks, or career ladder design — undermines both the DE and the managers it is conflated with.

⟳
GenAI COE Partnership

PT's DE and the GenAI COE Senior Director must have a defined, collaborative relationship — not a siloed one. The DE shapes how PT builds on GenAI capabilities; the COE shapes what those capabilities are. Neither is effective without the other.

◎
Defined Technical Mandate

The DE's mandate must be articulated at the domain level — not scoped to a single application or team's architecture. Application-level scope is Sr. Lead Engineer work. Domain-level scope is DE work. The difference is not seniority; it is aperture.

These conditions are not aspirational comfort items. They are the difference between a DE who delivers transformational technical impact for PT and one who is underutilized in a mismatched role. The framework benchmark itself states: a DE "drives strategic transformational outcomes across a domain and supporting functions." That requires domain-level access and domain-level mandate.

05

What Good Looks Like — Success Metrics for the PT DE

The IFX DE benchmark is explicit: results must be tangible, traceable to the DE's direct contribution, and demonstrated every performance cycle. Alignment, strategy, and cultural work count only insofar as they lead to measurable technical outcomes. The following metrics translate that standard into PT's specific context.

  • 01
    Platform Architecture — Hands-On Delivery

    High-quality code reviews, merged PRs, or direct architectural contributions on PT's shared internal platforms — with documented impact on reliability, scalability, or developer efficiency. Not advisory; contributory.

  • 02
    Standards Adoption

    Enterprise architecture standards and integration patterns defined or updated by the DE that are then followed by PT Domain Architects — measurable by BRLR compliance rates, reduction in architectural exceptions, and anti-pattern recurrence.

  • 03
    AI/ML Platform Progression

    Architectural decisions on PT's AI/ML platform that are validated in production and deliver traceable business or workforce value — not PoCs that don't land, but decisions that result in shipped capability.

  • 04
    Cross-Domain Coherence

    Measurable reduction in integration redundancy and arbitrary uniqueness across HRTech, CorpTech, and HRBP — evidenced by retiring duplicate systems, adopting common patterns, or reducing support load through architectural simplification.

  • 05
    IC Development

    Tangible growth outcomes for engineers mentored through DE 100/200 and within PT — promotions, technical capability uplift, or expansion of engineering scope attributable in part to DE mentorship and coaching.

  • 06
    Incident & Risk Mitigation

    Architecture decisions or standards that prevent production incidents, reduce compliance exposure, or produce positive findings from Risk, Cyber, Audit, or Legal — where the DE's involvement was material to the outcome.

06

The Senior Distinguished Engineer in PeopleTech

The DE role defined in this paper operates at the PT domain level. As the org scales, a Senior Distinguished Engineer (SDE) operating at the IFX LOB level is the natural next step in PT's technical leadership architecture.

The distinction between DE and SDE is not one of seniority alone — it is one of aperture. The DE's mandate is PT-wide: setting technical standards, owning the AI/ML platform architecture, ensuring cross-domain coherence across HRTech, CorpTech, and HRBP. The SDE's mandate is IFX-wide: representing PT's technical voice in enterprise conversations, shaping the technical strategy that PT's DE then executes, and serving as the engineering counterpart to the IFX MVP in credibility and influence.

How the DE and SDE Roles Relate

These roles are not redundant — they operate at different levels of abstraction and at different organizational altitudes. The DE is the most senior technical voice within PT. The SDE is the most senior technical voice for PT in the broader IFX and enterprise context.

Dimension Distinguished Engineer (DE) Senior Distinguished Engineer (SDE)
Primary Scope PeopleTech — HRTech, CorpTech, HRBP, AI/ML Platform IFX LOB level — PT, MLX, RiskTech, FinanceTech
Technical Strategy Defines and owns PT's domain architecture strategy and standards Shapes IFX-level technical strategy; inputs to enterprise architecture direction
Reporting Altitude Senior Director or above within PT MVP or Chief Architect at IFX level
External Representation Represents PT in IFX architecture forums and cross-LOB communities Represents IFX in enterprise and industry-level technical forums
Relationship to DE Sets the technical direction the SDE amplifies externally Sets the IFX-wide context within which the DE operates; a force multiplier, not a manager
Talent Development Mentors Domain Architects and ICs within PT; DE 100/200 programs Mentors DEs across IFX LOBs; shapes the IFX DE career framework itself
Innovation Horizon 3–12 months — current platform decisions, standards adoption, architectural coherence 12–36 months — emerging technology bets, IFX-wide platform investments, enterprise capability gaps

What the SDE Role Enables for PT Specifically

PT's domain — HR, corporate technology, workforce AI — sits at the intersection of every other Capital One LOB. Every team's people processes, performance systems, hiring infrastructure, and corporate tooling touch PT. An SDE in PT therefore has disproportionate enterprise leverage compared to an SDE in a more narrowly scoped LOB.

Specifically, a PT SDE would be positioned to:

⬡
Represent PT at IFX Architecture Forums

Ensure PT's technical decisions are visible and influential at the IFX level — not just consumed downstream of decisions made elsewhere in IFX.

⟡
Bridge PT and the Enterprise GenAI Strategy

PT's AI/ML platform for HR and corporate use cases is uniquely positioned to inform Capital One's broader GenAI workforce strategy. An SDE gives that work enterprise-level visibility and sponsorship.

◫
Set the Technical Bar for PT's DE Career Path

The SDE defines what DE-level excellence looks like within IFX, giving the PT DE a visible and credible progression target — and giving PT a mechanism to develop and retain senior IC talent.

⊞
Drive Cross-LOB Reuse from PT's Platforms

PT builds platforms consumed enterprise-wide. An SDE can actively drive adoption of PT's common capabilities across other IFX LOBs — converting internal platforms into genuine enterprise assets.

On sequencing: The SDE role is most effective when the DE role beneath it is well-defined and structurally supported. Hiring an SDE into a PT without a clear DE framework risks repeating the same structural ambiguity at a higher level. This paper establishes the DE foundation deliberately — so that the SDE, when hired, has a clear mandate to build on rather than a gap to fill.

07

Looking Ahead — Recommendations for PT Leadership

PT is at an inflection point in its technical maturity. The organization builds platforms consumed enterprise-wide, is expanding into AI/ML capabilities, and operates in a domain with significant compliance and integration complexity. This is the moment to establish the structural conditions for sustained technical leadership — not reactively, but intentionally.

  • Formalize the DE Role Definition for PT

    Adopt this paper and the companion RACI as PT's official DE role framework. Socialize with IFX leadership to ensure PT's DE definition aligns with and contributes to the broader IFX engineering talent architecture. This creates the "common language" the IFX benchmark framework calls for.

  • Establish the Structural Conditions

    Review the six structural conditions outlined in Section 04 against PT's current org design. Identify which conditions are in place, which are partially met, and which require deliberate action. Prioritize reporting altitude and cross-pillar access as the two highest-leverage changes.

  • Define the DE–GenAI COE Engagement Model

    Establish a formal, recurring engagement between the PT DE and the GenAI COE Senior Director. Clarify the boundary between PT's AI/ML platform direction (DE accountability) and GenAI COE common capability delivery (COE accountability). Ambiguity here creates duplication and missed leverage.

  • Consolidate the Architecture Role Model

    Retire the multi-tiered architect persona model (Domain, Application, Solution, Experience Architects as separate tiers) in favor of the two-role model described in this paper: Distinguished Engineer and Domain/Application Architect. This simplification reduces role confusion, clarifies accountability, and makes the DE's cross-domain mandate unambiguous.

  • Define and Activate the SDE Role

    Use Section 06 of this paper as the foundation for the SDE role definition in PT. Establish the DE-to-SDE relationship, reporting altitude, and IFX-level mandate before or concurrent with any SDE hire — so the incoming SDE has structural clarity from day one.

  • Build a PT DE Career Ladder

    Define what progression looks like for individual contributors on the DE track within PT — from Sr. Lead Engineer to DE, and from DE to SDE. Use the IFX benchmark framework as the foundation, with PT-specific examples in each pillar. This creates a path that retains technical talent and makes PT's engineering ambitions legible to prospective candidates.

A note on timing: PT currently has one Distinguished Engineer. The structural decisions made now — how the role is scoped, how it is positioned, what access it has — will determine whether that investment compounds or stagnates. The IFX benchmark is clear that a DE should demonstrate tangible technical impact every performance cycle. That is only possible if the structural conditions for impact are in place.
